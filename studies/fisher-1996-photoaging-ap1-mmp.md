# Molecular basis of sun-induced premature skin ageing and retinoid antagonism

Fisher GJ, Datta SC, Talwar HS, Wang ZQ, Varani J, Kang S, Voorhees JJ · *Nature* 1996;379:335–339 · doi:10.1038/379335a0 · PMID: 8552187

## TL;DR

In a human in vivo study, a single dose of UVB irradiation applied to adult buttock skin induced three matrix-degrading metalloproteinases (MMP-1, MMP-3, MMP-9) within hours, while MMP-2 was not induced. Induction was dose-dependent starting at 0.01 MED — far below the threshold for skin reddening. The transcription factors AP-1 (Jun/Fos) and NF-κB were activated within minutes at 2 MED and at sub-erythemogenic doses, preceding and likely driving MMP transcription. Topical all-trans retinoic acid (t-RA) pretreatment reduced UVB-induced AP-1 DNA binding by 70% and reduced MMP-1 (interstitial collagenase) and MMP-9 (92K gelatinase) mRNAs, proteins, and activities by 50–80%, via protein-protein transrepression of AP-1 rather than as a sunscreen effect. This paper established the molecular mechanism connecting UV exposure to collagen degradation in photoaging and the molecular basis for retinoid anti-aging therapy.

---

## Background and Hypothesis

The authors noted that damage to skin collagen and elastin is the hallmark of long-term solar UV irradiation exposure and is believed to be responsible for the wrinkled appearance of sun-exposed skin (introduction, p. 335). Prior work had shown that metalloproteinases degrade collagenous and non-collagenous ECM components in skin, but induction of these enzymes by UV in human skin in vivo had not been directly demonstrated. AP-1 and NF-κB were known stimulators of metalloproteinase gene transcription, and all-trans retinoic acid was known to transrepress AP-1. The study tested whether UVB activates this cascade in living human skin and whether t-RA could block it.

## Methods

### Subjects and UV Protocol

- **Subjects:** Adult Caucasians with light to mild pigmentation, approximately equal numbers of males and females (exact sex breakdown not reported per subject group; described in Fig. 1 legend as "adult Caucasians")
- **Skin site:** Buttock skin (photoprotected site, providing baseline unirradiated tissue)
- **UV source:** Ultralite Panelite lamp with four F36T12 ERE-VHO UVB tubes; intensity monitored with an IL443 phototherapy radiometer and SED240/UVB/W photodetector; output 0.5 mW cm⁻² at 48 cm
- **Dose unit:** Minimal erythema dose (MED) determined individually for each subject at 24 h post-irradiation; ranged 30–50 mJ cm⁻² across subjects
- **Primary irradiation dose:** 2 MED UVB (for most time-course experiments)
- **Tissue collection:** Skin punched by keratome at indicated time points from irradiated and adjacent non-irradiated sites; snap-frozen

### Assays

- **mRNA:** Northern blot; bands quantified by PhosphorImager; normalized to control gene 36B4; results expressed as fold increase over non-irradiated skin
- **Protein and activity:** Interstitial collagenase measured by western blot (100 μg protein per lane, chemiluminescence) and by hydrolysis of [³H] fibrillar collagen (100 μg per assay). 92K gelatinase measured by western blot and gelatin zymography (20 μg per assay). Band intensities quantified by laser densitometry.
- **AP-1 and NF-κB binding:** Electrophoretic mobility-shift assay (EMSA); 8 μg nuclear extract protein; ³²P-labelled double-stranded DNA probes; AP-1 normalized to SP-1 (which did not change with UVB at any dose or time point — served as internal control); antibody supershifts confirmed Jun/Fos (anti-Jun, anti-Fos broad reactivity) and p65/Rel A (anti-p65 specific)
- **Stromelysin I vs II discrimination:** Northern blot hybridization with (a) stromelysin I-specific probe; (b) full-length stromelysin I probe; (c) stromelysin II-specific probe. Signal observed with (a) and (b); no signal with (c) — explicitly confirming stromelysin I, not stromelysin II
- **Skin reddening:** Minolta chromameter CR200 (model 94401085); relative reddening vs non-irradiated skin

## Findings

### 1. UVB Induces MMP-1, MMP-3, and MMP-9 mRNA in Human Skin In Vivo (Fig. 1)

At 2 MED UVB, interstitial collagenase (MMP-1; 2.6 kb band, normalized by 36B4), stromelysin I (MMP-3; 1.9 kb), and 92K gelatinase (MMP-9; 2.8 kb) mRNAs were all induced. Time course:

- **MMP-1 (interstitial collagenase):** Peaked at 16–24 h post-UVB (~60–80-fold increase visible by bar graph reading in Fig. 1a), declined toward baseline by 48–72 h
- **MMP-3 (stromelysin I):** Induction pattern parallel to MMP-1 (Fig. 1b)
- **MMP-9 (92K gelatinase):** Induced with similar time course (Fig. 1c); rose ~6–8-fold at peak (Fig. 1c bars)
- **72K gelatinase (MMP-2):** "was detectable but was only elevated 1.6-fold at 24 hours post-UVB (Fig. 1d)"; paper states further that "neither 72 gelatinase protein nor enzyme activity was induced" (p. 337) — **MMP-2 is explicitly stated as NOT induced by UVB**

**The stromelysin I / stromelysin II distinction:** Northern blot hybridization with the stromelysin II-specific probe "yielded no signal" (p. 336 legend, Fig. 1), explicitly confirming that it is stromelysin I (MMP-3), not stromelysin II (MMP-10), that is induced by UVB. This distinction is frequently confused in secondary literature.

Both mRNA time courses and protein/activity measurements paralleled each other (stated explicitly in text, p. 337: "Time courses for induction of interstitial collagenase and 92K gelatinase proteins and activities by 2 MED UVB paralleled those observed for their mRNAs").

All values are means ± SEM; P < 0.05 vs no-UVB control, two-tailed paired t-test.

### 2. MMP-1 and MMP-9 Induction is Dose-Dependent, Starting at 0.01 MED (Fig. 2)

At 24 h post-UVB, dose-dependent induction of interstitial collagenase (MMP-1) protein and activity and 92K gelatinase (MMP-9) protein and activity was observed across the range 0–2 MED (Fig. 2a, b). Key thresholds (from text p. 337):

- **Interstitial collagenase was induced by all doses of UVB tested (0.01–2 MED)**
- **92K gelatinase was induced by ≥0.1 MED**
- "Induction of both interstitial collagenase and 92K gelatinase was maximal at 1 MED and approximately half-maximal at 0.1 MED" (text, p. 337)
- 0.1 MED is explicitly described as "equivalent to 2–3 min solar irradiation on a summer day, which causes no perceptible skin reddening" (text, p. 337)

Western blots in Fig. 2a inset show two bands for interstitial collagenase: 54K (intact) and 45K (proteolytically activated form) — both elevated with UVB.

n = 10 per dose; * P < 0.025 vs no-UVB control.

### 3. AP-1 and NF-κB Are Activated Within Minutes and at Sub-Erythemogenic Doses (Fig. 3)

**Specificity confirmation (Fig. 3a):** AP-1 retarded complexes were supershifted by anti-Jun and anti-Fos antibodies; NF-κB complexes were supershifted by anti-p65/Rel A antibody. Competition with mutant labelled probes confirmed binding specificity. SP-1 binding did not vary between non-irradiated and irradiated samples at any time point — served as internal control.

**Time course (Figs 3b, 3c) at 2 MED:**
- AP-1 binding: induced by 15 min post-UVB, remained elevated at least through 4 h (measured timepoints: No UVB, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4 h; * P < 0.01 vs no-UVB at all measured timepoints except the first); stated in text: "remained elevated for at least 24 hours (data not shown)"
- NF-κB binding: similar rapid induction kinetics (Fig. 3c); "remained elevated for at least 24 hours" (text)
- Induction was approximately 2.5–3-fold at peak by bar graph reading (Figs 3b, 3c)

**Dose-response (Fig. 3d):**
- Both AP-1 and NF-κB binding were dose-dependent from 0 to 2 MED
- "Half-maximal induction of AP-1 and NF-κB, measured 30 min after irradiation, occurred at ~0.1 MED and maximal induction at 1 MED" (text, p. 338)
- Significant at 0.01 MED: "The UVB dose dependencies for induction of AP-1 and NF-κB closely matched those observed for induction of interstitial collagenase and 92K gelatinase (Fig. 2), consistent with participation of AP-1 and NF-κB in UVB-induced increases in these metalloproteinases." (text, p. 338)
- Note: the text states significant dose-response starting at 0.01 MED (Fig. 3d legend: * P < 0.02 vs no-UVB control)

n = 9 per timepoint/dose.

### 4. t-RA Pretreatment: Canonical Extraction of the ~70% and 50–80% Figures (Fig. 4)

**CANONICAL RESOLUTION OF R38 VERIFIER CONFLICT:**

Both the ~70% AP-1 reduction and the 50–80% MMP reduction figures are **explicitly stated in the paper text** (p. 338), not solely derivable from visual figure estimation. The exact quotes:

> "t-RA pretreatment also reduced UVB-induced AP-1 DNA binding by 70% (Fig. 4a)." — text, p. 338

> "t-RA pretreatment also reduced UVB-induced 92K gelatinase... mRNAs, proteins and activities by 50–80%." — text, p. 338 (referring to Fig. 4b)

> "t-RA pretreatment also reduced... interstitial collagenase (Fig. 4c) mRNAs, proteins and activities by 50–80%." — text, p. 338

These statements are in the body text of the paper on page 338, in the paragraph beginning "Pretreatment of skin, which expresses retinoic acid receptors, with all-trans retinoic acid (t-RA) reduced UVB-induced AP-1 DNA binding by 70%..."

**Additional text-confirmed detail:**
- 72K gelatinase (MMP-2) mRNA, protein, and activity "were not induced by UVB, were also not altered by t-RA (data not shown)" — text, p. 339
- t-RA did NOT reduce UVB-induced skin reddening at any dose (Fig. 4d; differences between vehicle- and t-RA-treated skin were not statistically significant; n = 7); this explicitly rules out a sunscreen/absorption-based mechanism
- Glucocorticoid (clobetasol propionate 0.05%) also reduced UVB-induced interstitial collagenase and 92K gelatinase activities (Fig. 4e; * P < 0.01 vs vehicle; n = 7 collagenase, n = 8 gelatinase) — provides independent pharmacological confirmation that AP-1/NF-κB are the relevant upstream drivers

**What is confirmed from figure visual estimation only (not explicitly stated in text):**
- The absolute bar heights in Figs 4b and 4c (vehicle vs t-RA) are readable for directional comparison but the 50–80% range is confirmed by the text statement; no separate single-number reduction for each of mRNA/protein/activity for each MMP is given in the text (only the 50–80% range applying collectively to mRNA, protein, and activity for each)
- The Fig. 4a gel-shift image shows a clear reduction in the AP-1 band in the RA+UVB lane vs V+UVB lane; the 70% figure is from the bar graph quantification (also directly stated in text)

**Protocol for t-RA experiments:** 0.1% t-RA or its vehicle applied to subjects for 48 h before irradiation with 2 MED UVB; skin obtained 30 min later for AP-1 measurements, or 24 h later for metalloproteinase measurements.

### 5. Mechanism: AP-1 Transrepression via Protein-Protein Interaction (text, p. 338–339)

The paper explicitly states the mechanism:

> "Transrepression is ligand-dependent, and apparently involves protein–protein interactions between hormone receptors and Jun or Fos." (text, p. 338)

These interactions create complexes lacking transactivation capacity for either AP-1 or hormone-response elements.

**What the paper does NOT invoke as the t-RA mechanism:**
- CBP/p300 competition — not mentioned
- TIMP-1 induction — not mentioned
- Sunscreen/UV-absorption effects — explicitly ruled out (no reduction in skin reddening; Fig. 4d)

The 72K gelatinase (MMP-2) also does not contain AP-1 or NF-κB response elements in its promoter (text, p. 338, citing ref. 16) — explaining why it is neither induced by UVB nor suppressed by t-RA.

Glucocorticoid data (Fig. 4e) support the AP-1/NF-κB transrepression hypothesis because glucocorticoid receptors are also known to transrepress AP-1, and glucocorticoid pretreatment similarly reduced MMP induction "to extents similar to t-RA" (text, p. 339).

## Limitations and Caveats

- **Single UV dose per time point per subject** — each subject's skin site was irradiated once; the study design does not capture cumulative repeated low-dose UV effects that characterise lifetime photoaging
- **Buttock skin only** — photoprotected site; may not fully represent habitually sun-exposed facial or dorsal-hand skin where photoaging is clinically apparent
- **Short follow-up** — mRNA/protein time courses extend to 72 h post-UVB; long-term consequences of repeated AP-1/MMP induction cycles are inferred, not measured in this study
- **No collagen degradation endpoint** — the study documents MMP mRNA, protein, and enzyme activity; actual collagen peptide release or net collagen loss was not measured in these experiments
- **All-Caucasian cohort** — AP-1/NF-κB responses may differ in higher-melanin phototypes; not addressed
- **Number of subjects per measurement varies** — n = 6–17 across experiments; some endpoints are from relatively small groups (n = 7 for some collagenase activity measurements)
- **MED range 30–50 mJ cm⁻²** — substantial inter-subject variability in MED; all results are normalized to individual MED for comparability
- **t-RA skin reddening experiment** — the finding that t-RA does NOT reduce UV-induced reddening (Fig. 4d) was used to rule out sunscreen effects; however, t-RA absorption spectrum overlaps with UVB. The authors argue the reduction in AP-1/MMP was "specific, rather than being due to absorption of UVB by t-RA" because reddening was not reduced, but this indirect argument, while persuasive, was not accompanied by direct photon-flux measurement at skin with/without t-RA

## Significance and Aging-Field Impact

This paper (1,395 citations; 100th percentile FWCI) established two durable mechanistic frameworks that have shaped the photoaging and anti-aging dermatology field:

1. **The UV → AP-1/NF-κB → MMP → collagen-degradation cascade** as the core molecular mechanism of photoaging in humans — demonstrated directly in human skin in vivo, not in cell culture or animal models
2. **The molecular basis for retinoid anti-aging therapy** — tretinoin works by AP-1 transrepression (protein–protein interaction between RAR and Jun/Fos), not as a collagen-synthesis stimulator (that downstream benefit, involving TGF-β/Smad3, is described in later Fisher/Voorhees lab work, not in this paper)

The demonstration that MMP induction occurs at sub-erythemogenic doses (0.01 MED — visually imperceptible) provided the first mechanistic explanation for why cumulative lifetime sun exposure causes premature aging even in individuals who avoid sunburn. This finding underpins all subsequent sun-protection-as-anti-aging recommendations.

The negative result for MMP-2 (explicitly not induced by UVB, and not affected by t-RA) has been under-appreciated in secondary literature; MMP-2 is constitutively expressed in fibroblasts and participates in baseline matrix remodelling, but its regulation is distinct from the UV-driven AP-1/NF-κB-dependent MMPs.

## Gaps

- `#gap/needs-replication` — the t-RA effect on AP-1 binding and MMP induction was shown with a single t-RA dose regimen (48 h pretreatment, 0.1%); dose-response for t-RA itself was not characterised in this paper; subsequent clinical-trial literature (Griffiths 1995, Weiss 1988 — not extracted here) addresses clinical endpoints but not necessarily this specific molecular read-out
- `#gap/long-term-unknown` — the paper characterises the acute MMP response to a single UV dose; whether a t-RA regimen prevents cumulative collagen loss from repeated UV exposures over years was not assessed here
- `#gap/no-mechanism` — the upstream sensor converting UVB photon absorption into AP-1/NF-κB activation within minutes is not characterised in this paper; subsequent work implicated mitochondrial ROS and EGFR transactivation, but those mechanisms are not in this paper
